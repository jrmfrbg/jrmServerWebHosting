The Cartography of Belonging

Exploring migration, memory, and the cross-cultural "exterior" of Kit de Waal's Smethwick.

The Inhabitants

People are not furniture polish. The narrative centers on characters navigating the friction between commercial value and personal history.

A

Alfonse Maynard

A St. Kitts migrant who represents the Windrush generation's struggle. His identity is defined by a "heart-to-heart" with Malcolm X, giving him the agency to "stand up and be a man."

Core Value: Dignity
L

Lillian (Lily)

The late wife who crossed the racial divide of the 1960s. Her absence is the "ghost" in the house that the agent seeks to scrub away with beeswax and air freshener.

Core Value: Memory
B

Mike Baxter

The agent of gentrification. He views the "red door" as a sales tactic rather than a symbol of survival. He represents the contemporary flattening of history into "assets."

Core Value: Utility

The Crucible of 1965

The story is anchored in the historical reality of Smethwick's Marshall Street—a place Malcolm X called a "hotbed of racial tension." The Council's attempt to buy houses to enforce segregation serves as the antagonistic force that Alfonse must resist.

Institutional Exclusion Index

"He spoke heart to heart."

The encounter with Malcolm X is the narrative's fulcrum. It transforms Alfonse's relationship with the street from one of fear to one of defiant ownership.

The Colour Bar

The "Blue Gates" pub represents the physical borders of 1960s Britain. Lillian’s romance with Alfonse was an act of "border-crossing" in a segregated landscape.

The Semiotics of the Red Door

Analyzing how the same physical object carries different meanings for the agent and the inhabitant.

1

Exterior Paint vs. Interior Truth

Baxter sees the red door as a way to hide a "shoddy porch." Alfonse sees the paint under his nails as the labor of belonging. The door is a skin that protects the "smell of people" inside.

2

The Scent of Memory

The front rooms are a stage set for the white gaze (beeswax/air freshener). The back room is the Caribbean heart—smelling of thyme, pepper, and white rum.
